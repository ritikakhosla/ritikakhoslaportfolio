UX Labs at SuiteWorld

Evaluating a new troubleshooting workflow for the NetSuite Shopify Connector through in-person usability testing at Oracle's flagship annual conference — driving design improvements ahead of the December release.

Role
Lead Researcher & Facilitator
Methods
Usability Testing · Content Highlighter
Team
1 PM · 2 Designers · 2 Engineers · 2 Tech Writers
Timeline
3 months · Aug–Oct 2024
Background

A critical workflow for NetSuite's Shopify Connector

NetSuite's Shopify Connector is one of the most widely used integrations in the platform. In an upcoming December 2024 release, the Connector team was introducing a new troubleshooting workflow and desired greater confidence in its usability and experience.

Our team had visionary designs in place (informed by a prior research round). My goal was to evaluate whether the solution delivered its intended user value: a clear, complete, and satisfying self-service troubleshooting experience.

Scoping

Aligning a team with competing priorities

My first challenge was stakeholder alignment. The team had varying priorities — some wanted to test whether the solution scaled, others focused on language and visual elements. I led a series of KUA and alignment workshops to build a shared research goal.

Research Goal: Evaluate the NetSuite-Shopify connector troubleshooting flow and novel help panel to ensure it is clear, complete, and self-explanatory for users.

I then selected two complementary methods: a usability test to evaluate ease-of-use and satisfaction across the workflow, and a content highlighter activity to surface what language in the help guide was useful, missing, or unnecessary.

Research Design

Designing for in-person sessions at SuiteWorld

I designed three task flows tested in sequence, each with quantitative metrics (SEQ, UMUX-LITE, CSAT) alongside think-aloud observation:

01

Discovering the error

Participants logged in and identified a connection problem with Shopify. Measured via First Click Test and SEQ.

02

Troubleshooting with the guided panel

Participants walked through the guided solution panel to resolve the error. Measured via SEQ, UMUX-LITE, and CSAT.

03

Performing a health check

Participants confirmed the Shopify connection was restored. Measured via SEQ, UMUX-LITE, and CSAT.

04

Content highlighter

Participants marked sections of the help guide as confusing, useful, or unnecessary — surfacing specific language improvements.

I ran a moderated pilot with an internal SME before SuiteWorld, which led to 2 design improvements and 1 task flow adjustment for smoother prototype flow and time management.

Key Findings

Users struggled to initiate the help panel — but once open, found the instructions clear and easy to follow

01

Errors understood, navigation friction high

Participants understood the cause of errors and the steps to fix them, but experienced consistent friction navigating between steps.

02

High satisfaction with the guided help drawer

Participants appreciated the help drawer that remained accessible as they moved between pages — and reported high overall satisfaction (CSAT: 5.75/7).

03

Desire for greater navigation flexibility

Users wanted more direct links and fewer clicks — they felt the troubleshooting path was too linear for the complexity of their workflows.

04

Help guide needed formatting improvements

The content highlighter surfaced consistent suggestions: a bulleted format and hyperlinks would significantly improve scannability.

Impact

The research findings directly informed design iterations, shaping the final workflow ahead of the December product release

I led post-research align-and-assign workshops where the team reviewed findings and took ownership of next steps, which ensured accountability and momentum going into the December release.

n=4In-person usability sessions at SuiteWorld

↗Multiple notification paths added to next-cycle roadmap
Reflections

What I learned

On small samples: With n=4 due to event constraints and participant drop-offs, I was deliberate about framing findings as directional — not deterministic. Reminding stakeholders to treat insights as guidance, not conclusion, preserved research integrity without undermining impact.
On competing priorities: I learned that workshops and cross-sharing on knowns, unknowns and assumptions helped narrow down the research focus. This kept the team grounded in user needs throughout a fast-moving process.

Card Sort Survey Design Oracle Apr–May 2025

Shaping GenAI Summaries

An unmoderated closed card sort and ranking survey to identify what information users want in a GenAI-powered customer record summary — directly shaping the June 2025 feature release.

Role
Lead Researcher
Methods
Card Sort · Survey · Ranking
Team
1 PM · 1 Designer · 2 Engineers · 2 Tech Writers
Timeline
2 months · Apr–May 2025
Background

A GenAI feature with no user grounding — yet

NetSuite's Customer Record is a central hub for sales representatives — where they document, manage, and review all interactions with a customer. In an upcoming June 2025 release, the team was adding a GenAI-powered summary to the page, designed to give reps a condensed view of key information and improve efficiency.

The feature addressed a known user need. But without research, the team had no clear answer to a critical question: what information do users actually want in that summary, and how should it be ordered?

Scoping

Navigating tight timelines with a lean, high-impact method

Team availability was limited due to an upcoming release season. I implemented a RACI framework to set clear expectations across stakeholders, then collaborated with the PM to apply MoSCoW prioritization — distinguishing what we must know before June from what could wait for a later cycle.

Research Goal: Identify the information most valuable to customers in a GenAI-generated summary on the Customer Record page, and determine how it should be presented for clarity and efficiency.

Given recruitment constraints and organizational priorities, I selected a quick but high-value approach: an unmoderated survey via DScout combining a closed card sort (to learn what content users want) and a ranking question (to determine preferred order and presentation).

Research Design

9 questions. 25 participants. Designed for statistical signal.

The survey comprised 9 questions across three areas: Customer Record usage and pain points, firmographic context, and the GenAI summary itself. I designed screening questions to help Research Ops target the right participants — NetSuite customers whose daily responsibilities tied to sales and who interacted with the Customer Record page daily or weekly.

Before launch, I ran a moderated pilot with two internal SMEs to catch interpretation issues — resulting in 1–2 wording improvements that ensured consistent understanding across respondents.

01

Closed card sort

Participants sorted information types into "must have," "nice to have," and "not needed" — revealing clear consensus and disagreement areas.

02

Ranking question

Participants ordered content types by preferred prominence in the summary. I calculated weighted average ranks and variance to surface consensus preferences.

03

Open-ended questions

Participants described what they liked, disliked, or found missing in an example summary — adding qualitative texture to quantitative patterns.

04

DScout agreement matrix

I used DScout's agreement matrix to assess the percentage of participants placing each item in the same category — providing statistical confidence for design decisions.

Key Findings

Financial data first. Support cases: useful, not essential.

01

Transactional data is non-negotiable

68% of users identified Sales Orders, Invoices, Payments, and Balance Information as must-have — anchoring the summary for daily decision-making.

02

Support cases: helpful, not required

Only 28% labeled Support Cases as essential, but 72% found them valuable when present — signaling they should be included but not front-loaded.

03

Financial & performance data leads

Users consistently ranked Billings/Financials and Sales Performance highest — these should appear first in any summary layout.

04

Communication & activity are secondary

No participants ranked Communication or Activity data first — contextually useful, but should not dominate the summary view.

Impact

User preferences shipped in June — and a new method adopted org-wide.

The June release incorporated these insights directly: the backend prompt was structured to surface Financial and Performance information first, reflecting user preferences. Beyond the feature itself, this study had lasting organizational impact.

n=25Participants — statistically meaningful for card sort
Jun '25Feature shipped with user-grounded content structure
50+Researchers reached via internal Craft of Research session
Organizational first: This was the first study at Oracle NetSuite's UXR team to use an unmoderated card sort. I led a knowledge-sharing session in the internal "Craft of Research" series — reaching 15+ researchers and increasing awareness of the method across a team of 50.
Reflections

What I learned

On non-traditional methods: Short timelines and limited Research Ops bandwidth pushed me toward an unmoderated card sort — a non-traditional choice for our team. That constraint became an opportunity: it moved quickly, delivered statistically meaningful signal, and introduced a new method to the org.
On survey fatigue: Combining a card sort and ranking in one survey risked participant fatigue and superficial responses. Running a moderated pilot and building an interpretation checklist for note-takers was essential — reinforcing the value of moderated testing before any unmoderated launch.

Research Democratization Program

Establishing a quarterly field visit program to embed user empathy across NetSuite's cross-functional teams in the Redwood Shores hub.

Role
Program Lead
Methods
Field Research · User Interviews· Mixed Emotion Activity
Outcome
Org-wide empathy program
Context
NetSuite · Redwood Shores
The Challenge

Non-research teams had little direct exposure to NetSuite customers

Our Product GVP raised that non-research teams have limited engagement with our end-users, and so he launced a program called 'Empathy at Scale'. I took lead at the Redwood Shores office to fundamentally change how non-researchers at NetSuite experiences user empathy.

Research Approach

Quarterly field visits — with cross-functional teams, not just researchers

I designed and led a quarterly program where cross-functional teams — including PMs, engineers, and designers — joined structured field visits to observe and interact with real users in their working environment.

Each visit was carefully facilitated: I prepared observation guides tailored to each attendee's role so they knew what to look for, ran live debrief sessions immediately after visits while observations were fresh, and translated the raw experience into structured insights the team could act on.

The behavioral science angle: There's a significant difference between reading a research finding and watching a user struggle in real time. My Penn training in behavioral decision-making informed how I structured these sessions to maximize emotional impact — and therefore behavior change — among the cross-functional attendees.
I designed and ran the field visit program for 200+ employees — building it from the ground up with structured training sessions, introductory customer calls, and quarterly on-site visits to local NetSuite offices in cohorts of 7.
Key Findings

What changed when teams went into the field

01

Assumptions dissolved quickly

Many PMs and engineers reported that field visits overturned long-held assumptions about how enterprise users actually performed tasks day-to-day.

02

Research requests increased

After field visits, PMs began proactively requesting user research earlier in their planning cycles — a leading indicator of cultural change.

Impact

Research as an org-wide capability, not a department

The program established a repeatable, scalable model for embedding user empathy across NetSuite's product teams. By making field research a shared cross-functional experience rather than a UX-only activity, the program shifted how the org valued and acted on user insights.

NetSuite Website Redesign

The first research collaboration between Oracle NetSuite's UXR and Marketing organizations. I interviewed 14 prospective users to uncover messaging gaps, navigational improvements, and design feedback.

Role
Lead Researcher
Methods
IDIs · Think-Aloud · Design Feedback 
Readout
18 attendees incl. GVP & Director of Marketing
Timeline
2 months · Jan–Feb 2026
Background

Pioneering a partnership between NetSuite’s Product UXR and Marketing Teams

The NetSuite Marketing team was undergoing a full website redesign and reached out to our UXR team for qualitative data to inform improvements across content, navigation, and design. This was the first-ever research collaboration between Oracle NetSuite's UXR and Marketing organizations — a partnership I helped create and structure from the ground up.

I worked directly with the Director of NetSuite Marketing, securing internal approval from our Research Director and budget from the Marketing team to run 14 external user interviews through a third-party recruiting vendor.

Scoping

Setting expectations across a brand-new partnership

Working with a team that had never partnered with UXR required careful expectation-setting upfront. The Marketing team was accustomed to quantitative reports — clean numbers, clear causality. I held a stakeholder alignment call early to explain what qualitative research can and can't tell you, and framed findings as directional signals rather than definitive conclusions.

To build and maintain trust throughout the project, I met weekly with the Marketing Director, sharing emerging insights and key highlights along the way. I also provided an early preview of the final report to set clear expectations around how qualitative findings would be structured and communicated.

Participant criteria: 14 non-NetSuite users recruited via LoneStar — currently evaluating or recently purchased a software solution (within 6 months), and either the evaluator or recommender of that solution. Nice-to-have: software, manufacturing, or retail industries, aligned with NetSuite's core customer base.
Research Design

60-minute IDIs mapping the real buyer journey

Each session was structured to move from the participant's own search behavior into direct website feedback — grounding their reactions in an authentic search context. 

01

Rapport & context

Participants walked me through a recent or ongoing software search — what they considered, what they trusted, and how they evaluated options.

02

Organic search simulation

Starting from a blank Google page, participants mimicked searching for NetSuite — revealing when and if the website entered their natural buying journey.

03

Homepage design feedback

Participants gave think-aloud feedback on the homepage — first impressions, clarity of messaging, and what drew or lost their attention.

04

Three task flows (SEQ)

Participants completed: request a demo, find their industry, and find a new product. Each was measured with a Single Ease Question (SEQ) score.

Key Findings

Content gaps, design friction, and a messaging problem for NetSuite Next

01

NetSuite.com is a second-stop destination for many

Users often arrive at NetSuite.com as a second stop after reviewing the AI summary, and so expect to find stronger messaging than what they have seen.  Hmany who clicked the sponsored link were instead taken directly to a form, leading to a frustrating experience.

02

The banner: appreciated but too fast

Users appreciated the condensed, short-scroll banner design. However, many flagged that it moved too quickly for them to absorb the information. They wanted to be in control of the banner's pace

03

Language mismatch on key CTAs

"Product Demo" didn't resonate — users interpreted it as a formal sales commitment. "Tour" felt lighter and more exploratory, aligning better with where early-stage buyers are mentally.

04

NetSuite Next messaging was confusing

Users were unsure whether NetSuite Next was a separate product or a layer on top of NetSuite. Its placement at the end of the product dropdown further signaled low priority — raising doubts about organizational commitment to it.

Impact

Research findings drove meaningful changes to both the design and content strategy, directly shaping the final redesign

The final readout was attended by 18 stakeholders including the GVP of Marketing, Director of Marketing, Analytics, and SEO. The report was very well received — and directly informed several design and content changes to the redesigned site. This project also marked a lasting partnership between NetSuite's Research and Marketing organizations, opening the door to ongoing collaboration and a continued stream of work.

Reflections

What I learned

On working with a new team: The biggest challenge wasn't the research — it was managing expectations across an org that had never worked with UXR before. Weekly touchpoints, a preview report, and framing findings as directional kept the partnership strong and the final readout from feeling like a surprise.
On synthesis with open-ended data: Unstructured design feedback generates a lot of noise. I stayed anchored to the Marketing team's original questions as my synthesis framework — a discipline that helped me separate signal from observation and deliver recommendations that were crisp enough for an executive audience.
