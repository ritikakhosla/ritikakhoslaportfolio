Usability Testing In-person Research Oracle Sep 2024

UX Labs at SuiteWorld

Designing and facilitating in-person design feedback sessions at Oracle's flagship annual conference to gather qualitative insights from real enterprise users.

Role
Lead Researcher & Facilitator
Methods
Usability Testing · Think-aloud
Outcome
Product roadmap impact
Context
Conference · In-person
The Challenge

Getting feedback from enterprise users — at scale, in person

SuiteWorld is Oracle's largest annual conference, bringing together thousands of NetSuite customers. For the UX team, it's a rare opportunity to access a concentrated group of real enterprise users in one place. The challenge was designing a research experience that could run continuously across multiple days, generating actionable insights without disrupting conference attendees.

Research Approach

A pop-up UX lab in the middle of a conference floor

I designed a moderated usability testing protocol optimized for a conference environment — sessions short enough to fit between talks, but structured enough to yield reliable data. I recruited participants on-site, screened for relevant user personas, and ran back-to-back sessions throughout the event.

Each session used a think-aloud protocol, with participants narrating their experience as they interacted with current and prototype features. I coordinated with the product and design team in real time so that interesting findings could be discussed same-day.

The cross-functional angle: Rather than running research in isolation, I brought product managers and designers into the observation room. Watching users struggle — live — is far more persuasive than any research report.

Key Findings

What users actually told us

01

Navigation confusion in complex workflows

Users consistently struggled to locate key features within multi-step enterprise workflows, suggesting the information architecture needed restructuring.

02

Terminology mismatches

Several labels and terms in the UI didn't match how users described their own tasks — a classic findability problem rooted in internal vs. user language.

03

High confidence in core features

The core reporting and dashboard features performed well, with users expressing strong satisfaction once they located them.

04

Mobile expectations rising

Multiple enterprise users expressed surprise that certain tasks required desktop access, signalling growing expectation for mobile parity.

From conference floor to product roadmap

The findings were synthesized into a prioritized insight report delivered to the product and design team within a week of the conference. Several usability issues identified at SuiteWorld were directly incorporated into the subsequent sprint planning cycle.

Card Sort Unmoderated Research Oracle Apr 2025

Shaping GenAI Summaries

An unmoderated card sort study to uncover users' mental frameworks for GenAI-generated summaries — informing how the product team approached a high-stakes AI feature design decision.

Role
Lead Researcher
Methods
Unmoderated Card Sort
Outcome
Informed GenAI feature design
Context
Remote · B2B Enterprise
The Challenge

How do enterprise users mentally model AI-generated summaries?

As Oracle integrated generative AI features into NetSuite, the product team faced a foundational question: how do enterprise users actually think about AI-generated content? Do they trust it? Do they expect it to be editable? Where does it belong in their workflow?

Without a clear understanding of users' mental models, any design decision risked creating friction or eroding trust in a feature with enormous potential.

✏️ Fill in: Add specifics about which product area this related to and what the team's initial hypothesis was.
Research Approach

An unmoderated card sort to reveal mental models at scale

I designed an unmoderated card sort study to understand how users categorized different types of AI-generated summary content — which elements they expected to be static vs. editable, trusted vs. verified, primary vs. supplementary.

Using an unmoderated approach allowed me to reach a larger, more diverse sample of enterprise users across geographies and roles without requiring scheduling overhead. I carefully designed the card categories and instructions to elicit genuine mental model data rather than surface-level preferences.

Why card sort for AI features? GenAI features are novel — users don't have established patterns to draw from. A card sort forces them to reveal their underlying assumptions rather than simply rating what's put in front of them.
✏️ Fill in: What tool did you use? How many participants? How long did the study run?
Key Findings

Surprising patterns in how users categorize AI content

01

Trust varied significantly by content type

Users were more willing to trust AI summaries for factual/numerical data than for qualitative or interpretive content, suggesting layered trust tiers are needed.

02

Editability was assumed, not optional

Nearly all participants categorized AI summaries as a starting point, not a finished output — with strong expectation to edit or override.

03

Placement matters more than labeling

Where the summary appeared in the interface had more impact on perceived trustworthiness than how it was labeled as AI-generated.

04

Role-based differences emerged

Finance users and operations users organized content very differently, pointing to a need for role-aware AI summary customization.

Impact

Research that shaped the AI design direction

The card sort findings provided the product team with a clear, evidence-based framework for how to design GenAI summary features — informing decisions around editability, placement, trust signals, and role-based customization.

Research Democratization Program

Establishing a quarterly field visit program to embed user empathy across Oracle's cross-functional teams — moving research out of the UX silo and into the organization's DNA.

Role
Program Lead
Methods
Field Research · Facilitation
Outcome
Org-wide empathy program
Context
Oracle · Redwood Shores
The Challenge

Research insights that never left the research team

One of the most persistent challenges in enterprise UX is research that generates insights but fails to change behavior across the org. Product managers, engineers, and marketers rarely have direct contact with users — leaving them to rely on secondhand summaries or their own assumptions.

The goal of this program was to change that structural problem — not just produce better research, but fundamentally change how non-researchers at Oracle experienced user empathy.

✏️ Fill in: What was the specific trigger for this program? Was there a moment or problem that prompted you to propose it?
Research Approach

Quarterly field visits — with cross-functional teams, not just researchers

I designed and led a quarterly program where cross-functional teams — including PMs, engineers, and designers — joined structured field visits to observe and interact with real users in their working environment.

Each visit was carefully facilitated: I prepared observation guides tailored to each attendee's role so they knew what to look for, ran live debrief sessions immediately after visits while observations were fresh, and translated the raw experience into structured insights the team could act on.

The behavioral science angle: There's a significant difference between reading a research finding and watching a user struggle in real time. My Penn training in behavioral decision-making informed how I structured these sessions to maximize emotional impact — and therefore behavior change — among the cross-functional attendees.
✏️ Fill in: How many people participated across the program? Which teams were involved? How many visits were conducted?
Key Findings

What changed when teams went into the field

01

Assumptions dissolved quickly

Many PMs and engineers reported that field visits overturned long-held assumptions about how enterprise users actually performed tasks day-to-day.

02

Empathy drove faster buy-in

Teams who had participated in field visits showed noticeably faster alignment during research readout sessions — they already understood the "why."

03

Research requests increased

After field visits, PMs began proactively requesting user research earlier in their planning cycles — a leading indicator of cultural change.

04

Cross-team language improved

Teams developed shared vocabulary around user needs, reducing friction in cross-functional conversations about product direction.

Impact

Research as an org-wide capability, not a department

The program established a repeatable, scalable model for embedding user empathy across Oracle's product teams. By making field research a shared cross-functional experience rather than a UX-only activity, the program shifted how the org valued and acted on user insights.

NetSuite Website Redesign

End-to-end research to identify navigation, messaging, and conversion gaps in NetSuite's marketing website — culminating in a strategic redesign recommendation presented directly to the Marketing Group Vice President.

Role
Lead Researcher
Methods
Competitive Analysis · Interviews · Heuristics
Stakeholder
Marketing GVP
Context
Oracle NetSuite · Marketing
The Challenge

A website that wasn't converting — but nobody knew why

NetSuite's marketing website serves as the top-of-funnel entry point for enterprise buyers — a high-stakes surface where first impressions directly impact revenue. Despite significant traffic, internal stakeholders had growing concerns about whether the site was effectively communicating NetSuite's value to its target audience of mid-market and enterprise buyers.

The ask was to bring a research-driven lens to the website: understand where it was falling short, why, and what a better experience could look like.

✏️ Fill in: What were the specific business signals that prompted this project? (e.g. drop-off rates, stakeholder concerns, competitor pressure)
Research Approach

A triangulated approach: users, heuristics, and the competitive landscape

I designed a multi-method research plan to approach the problem from three angles simultaneously.

Competitive analysis first — I conducted a systematic review of how NetSuite's direct competitors (Sage Intacct, Microsoft Dynamics, SAP Business One) structured their marketing websites, mapped their messaging hierarchies, and identified patterns in how they addressed enterprise buyer concerns around trust, complexity, and ROI.

Heuristic evaluation — I assessed the existing NetSuite site against established UX principles, documenting friction points in navigation, information hierarchy, call-to-action clarity, and page load flows that were likely causing drop-off.

User interviews — I spoke with prospective enterprise buyers and existing customers to understand how they navigated the site, what information they looked for, and where their confidence in the product either increased or eroded.

Presenting to a GVP: Knowing this research would reach the Marketing Group Vice President, I structured the synthesis to lead with business impact — not just usability findings. Every insight was framed in terms of its downstream effect on pipeline, conversion, or brand perception.
✏️ Fill in: How many competitors did you analyze? How many users did you interview? What tools or frameworks did you use?
Key Findings

Where the site was losing enterprise buyers

01

Value proposition was buried

The site led with product features rather than business outcomes — misaligned with how enterprise buyers make decisions. Competitors consistently led with ROI and business transformation narratives.

02

Navigation didn't reflect buyer journeys

The site's IA was organized by product category, but enterprise buyers think in terms of their own business problems. Navigation created friction for self-directed exploration.

03

Trust signals were underweighted

Enterprise buyers — especially at larger companies — look for proof of scale and reliability before engaging sales. Case studies, certifications, and customer logos were hard to find.

04

CTAs created premature pressure

Calls to action skewed heavily toward "request a demo" — a high-commitment ask for users still in early research. Lower-friction conversion paths were largely absent.

The Recommendation

A redesign strategy built around the buyer journey

I synthesized the research into a strategic redesign recommendation organized around three core principles: lead with business outcomes not features, restructure navigation around buyer problems not product categories, and introduce a layered conversion strategy that meets buyers at their level of intent.

The recommendation included annotated wireframe concepts, a prioritized list of changes by impact vs. effort, and a competitive benchmarking summary for context.

Impact

From research to the executive table

The project culminated in a direct presentation to Oracle NetSuite's Marketing Group Vice President — a signal that research findings had clear strategic, not just tactical, value. The research reframed the conversation from "what should we redesign" to "how should enterprise buyers experience NetSuite."
